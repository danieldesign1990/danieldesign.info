Design of B2B Services at Property Finder

Role

Senior Designer

Property Finder

Property Finder is an online service that includes a website and application and lists commercial and residential properties for sale and rent. The company makes money by charging for placing listings and connecting real estate agencies with potential buyers for new developments. It also earns commissions as a mortgage broker by introducing clients to banks.

Additionally, besides the advertisements, the site contains analytical information about the UAE market from the Dubai Land Department (DLD); specifically, it can help understand whether it is more profitable to buy an apartment with mortgage or to rent and save or which property is better as an investment; helps to assess the best places to live if you relocate with young children; explains how local laws work and how they can protect you; and many other useful things—making it a comprehensive service related to property selection and transaction processing.

Property Finder is a brand for individuals. PF Expert is a brand for the business segment.

Customers

B2C

Individuals

The service value for individuals is that they can find housing that suits their needs and budget, and the real estate agent communicating with them will provide professional assistance, honestly disclose all terms, and quickly prepare the necessary documents.

Individuals have different needs. For instance, an investor wants to see a complete catalog of all new developments to choose a reliable property with minimal risk and maximum return. A relocating person with a family wants to find economically affordable housing in the community that is better in terms of location to school and commute to work. Both should see honest and accurate information, including photos and prices, and the agents responding to their queries should provide quick and high-quality service, consult, disclose all conditions, and draft transparent contracts.

B2B

Brokerages

The service value for real estate agencies (brokerages) is that it provides a steady source of interested potential clients from which their transactions, commissions, and earnings are generated.

Brokerages should receive quality leads from the company, meaning potential clients who are actively interested in the property from the listings, that can be quickly and efficiently turned into profitable deals that benefit both sides. They should understand how to maximize the use of the service, see transparent results on their return on investment, and have the ability to find insights within their company and in the real estate market to make management decisions and choose effective development strategies.

Developers

The service value for developers is that it allows them to manage partner sales and stimulate demand when needed, that is, to sell properties as quickly as possible through partners they have contracts with and at a set budget level.

Developers need sales stimulation at the right times and for the right budget. They want to sell all their projects as quickly as possible and for the highest price. There are cases when this is not a problem, and the units under construction are snapped up like hotcakes, and there are opposite cases when developers need to connect more partner sales and promote lagging properties among buyers—and this still needs to be managed depending on demand.

Main roles in the Property Finder environment.

Design Limitations

The business model chosen by Property Finder’s stakeholders imposes constraints that the B2B design department must consider in their work.

Property Finder only works with B2B professionals regarding listing placements. That means if you want to post a listing, you must be a brokerage, register in the system, and pass compliance checks. A private individual, such as a property owner, cannot create their own advertisement for selling their apartment. This differentiates the platform from many other real estate classifieds. This model was chosen not by chance; firstly, its purpose was to minimize the incidence of fraud and false advertisements on the platform. Secondly, such requirements are mandated by UAE laws, where a property owner must work through a realtor when selling property—however, the company’s model also operates in Egypt, where there are no similar legislative requirements (and it has its niche, with fewer listings but considered more trustworthy).

The second limitation relates to financial policy. Placing listings on the classified operates on a subscription basis, where the brokerage must pay a certain amount in dirhams each month, and for this, they get internal currency called "credits". Credits are spent on listing placements. Unused credits expire at the end of each month. This model resembles paying for a mobile phone or internet service—you pay a fixed amount per month for a package of services, which includes a certain number of minutes and gigabytes; anything you don’t use expires at the beginning of the following month. This limitation is more controversial, as the real estate market is cyclical; in some months, you may need more credits for publications, in others, less, and if they expire, it becomes harder to plan work, and you may feel frustrated by the wasted funds. Also, the inconvenience of this model is that, for a rough understanding of ROI and planning a marketing budget, brokerages often need to convert dirhams into credits and back. This model is because Property Finder wants to maintain a stable and maximally high revenue stream.

It should be noted that this model is strict; it has evolved over the years, and many things are tied to it. I can only, as a designer, highlight its disadvantages for the brokerage and advantages for the Property Finder, but not decide whether to use it. Of course, I should consider it in my work to smooth out its inconveniences as much as possible or even turn them into advantages, for example, by helping to utilize maximum credits in a month with high ROI to create a win-win situation.

PF Expert

PF Expert is a service for B2B clients from Property Finder. This service has its own branding, which uses blue-violet as the primary color, and its website design differs from a similar site for individual consumers. This situation is partly because the company wanted to separate branding for individuals from professionals and partly due to legacy—once PF Expert began to be made on the basis of Backbone JS, it used components something similar to Material Design different from B2C style. Now, we are smoothly converting it to React Native components, simultaneously developing a new service design for a smooth transition, matching as many components as possible while maintaining different branding for B2B and B2C (that is a business requirement).

The difference between website design for individuals and for business clients.

In the communications design for B2C users, there is no hard and fast rule to use only the primary red color, "clay." A secondary blue-violet color is also used for variety and emphasis. However, since there is less B2B communication design, we mostly use blue-violet color as the main color.

Roles in B2B Brokerage

A common brokerage model in the UAE consists of three main roles.

The Manager (Decision Maker) decides on investing in a new source of leads, signs documents, wants to see the results of their agents’ work, the payback of the service, and useful insights into the real estate market.

The Agent works in the field. They meet with buyers and sellers, photograph and show apartments, negotiate, and close deals.

The Admin is an office employee who receives photos and other terms from the agents, based on which they create, publish, and monitor listings. They also compile sales funnel reports for the management.

Agent—Admin interaction is the result of labor division in local brokerages, which partly developed because a) someone in authority needs to decide which listings are worth costly promotions, and this is better done by a person who sees the full picture of the list of ads across all agents, b) agents have limited opportunities to publish and verify their listing on the go. In any case, this does not mean that such interaction is always present; in small companies, agents perform the admin role and need a tool for convenient on-the-go listing management.

I need to mention that, depending on the brokerage model, there are also additional roles. For example, several managers might be at different hierarchical levels. One might be authorized to sign the contracts and pay for the service, and the other might not. An agent coach might need to see the leaderboard but does not allocate possible credits for use among agents. Somewhere, a finance person needs to account for written-off funds as a company asset and have access to this information, and so on.

It was necessary to create a role model that would satisfy the needs of all types of businesses maximally so that every brokerage, both large with many offices and small with a couple of agents, would find the system convenient to use without unnecessary or especially complex settings.

Research, Feedback, Analytics

One of the tasks of design is the correct use of information for decision-making. We conduct searches and identify the set of data that needs to be measured to have a complete picture of the customer experience and to make informed decisions: determining the direction of development, prioritizing, creating new features, and evaluating the achievement of set goals. This is not a trivial task—it involves using a large pool of information correctly to have a comprehensive understanding of the customer experience and how it reflects in the company’s final results.

Structure of research projects in Dovetail.

When designers and product owners plan tasks for the upcoming quarter, we rely on information sources, management priorities, and our professional vision. Information sources enable us to make informed decisions and then track their success. For example, we first monitor tickets created by account managers, which highlight the most painful issues that sometimes concern not only the technical side of the service but also problems with user experience. Then we rely on research that confirms or refutes hypotheses about product development. We also consider information obtained from surveys, feedback forms, comments from the App Store and Google Market, and comprehensive analysis of user behavior within the service.

Sources of Information:

  • Business Analytics. (Tableau);

  • Web Analytics. (Google Analytics, Microsoft Clarity);

  • Customer feedback through their account managers;

  • Customer feedback via chat, feedback forms, surveys;

  • Reviews under our app in the App Store, Google Play;

  • Research, In-depth interviews. (Dovetail);

  • Open market information from Dubai Land Department.

Setting Objectives and Measuring Outcomes

At the beginning of each quarter, every designer from the B2B department sets upcoming goals, about seventy percent of which are team goals and thirty percent are personal. Team goals depend on the team’s objectives, i.e., what they need to do to develop the product within their area of responsibility, while personal goals are related to their development as professionals or their contributions to the company that is not linked to the internal hierarchy. Typically, team goals involve designing new pages or features, while personal goals include taking courses, speaking at conferences, and developing the design system.

The product designer’s role in the company is strong. We participate in strategic sessions with product owners, managers, and analysts to determine service development priorities according to the leadership’s vision and requirements, legislative updates, the current or projected market situation, and collected feedback. The general scheme for setting goals is as follows: during the strategic session, teams define quarterly priorities based on the company’s high-level positioning, from which we derive clear and straightforward product design goals.

The general principle of design goals setting.

Each goal contains metrics by which its achievement is assessed. And sometimes, there is no starting point from which to measure behavioral stats. For example, when we did not have an introductory onboarding process, it was impossible to say how many steps were skipped or completed before its implementation—but it was possible to determine metric: the time from registering the first user to creating the first listing should be reduced. Also, sometimes, it’s difficult to determine the success of a decision based solely on web analytics. For instance, in the new block with the status of obtaining superagent (it’s a type of benefit for the broker) where just a number of sessions and time spent on the page will not allow us to understand how much it has become more valuable and clear—such cases require the collection of feedback. However, as a rule, there are metrics that are quite accurate; for example, when designing agent-client interactions, the goal was to reduce negative reviews on unavailable properties in listings, and the number of such reviews was the metric.

The design process in the team is structured as follows:

Each designer in the team is a senior specialist, meaning they can work independently. Therefore, after a task is defined, each designer decides what they need to complete it, which could be initial research, consultations with all stakeholders, or perhaps the preliminary information is sufficient, and they are ready to begin designing scenarios and interfaces. I should note that situations vary, so sometimes designers consult with me or colleagues to decide the most effective work sequence; other times, they need my assistance to defend the correct assessment of labor costs and work stages to product owners, who are always in a rush and tend to want designs without proper elaboration.

After the designer completes the final design, which may happen after several iterations of research and discussions with the product team, they send a link to this design with a brief description in our Slack channel. As the team is small, every member must find time to do a complete review of their colleague’s project in Figma, evaluate scenarios, the accuracy of breakpoints and templates, the usability of components, overall consistency, and so on. Once the project passes our internal review, the designer can move forward, prepare keys for localizing the design, make versions for other countries, and convey the layouts to the developers.

General scheme of the design process in the team.

After the layouts are handed over to development, there is a design review step on the test stand, where the designer checks how well the engineers’ work matches what they envisioned. Sometimes, compromises are made when minor flaws might be overlooked due to tight deadlines, but I do not encourage this, so we usually strive for perfect execution.

The designer’s work does not end there; after the design comes to life in the product, they must keep their finger on the pulse and check how seamless the user’s journey remains with the new functionality. They must also gather information about the applicability and value of the outcome of their work.

The main monitoring tools are Jira for tracking tasks, Lattice for tracking quarterly objects,
and Slack for communication and design reviews.

Arabic translation, adaptation in different countries

One of the company’s requirements for the product is its high-quality translation into Arabic and its proper adaptation in other countries of presence: Egypt, KSA, Qatar, and Bahrain. For this purpose, we use Localise, which creates a library of keys in two languages, and our engineers, in addition to pulling the necessary language keys, also change the direction of the content. Each page or project has a unique template for naming keys, following the rule "ProjectName_UniqueKeyName." The translation of these keys is done manually by the content team. This approach is not perfect; sometimes, in recordings, I see how a key in the Arabic version did not pull through or was not added, or I encounter issues with the direction for proper names or numbers, which we do not translate and leave in English execution. However, according to our colleagues from Egypt, this approach is much better than just automatic translation.

B2B team page in Localise.

In addition to creating keys and verifying their correct display in testing and production, the designer, in many cases, must prepare adapted versions of the interface for different countries. Initially, any design is made for our main market, the UAE, and once its final version passes the internal design review, the designer prepares layouts for other countries. Usually, this is not a complicated task, but it requires attention to detail. Since other countries have less functionality, for example, there are no compliance checks specific to Dubai, no verification of listings, consumer ratings, etc., the designer simply removes the relevant blocks or changes some fields of forms or widgets. As I already mentioned, the main difficulty here lies in meticulousness: it is necessary to carefully collect interface requirements for different countries from the product owner and present them in a simple form in Figma so that developers understand the differences, which should not deviate from the description tasks in Jira.

Design of B2B Services

Point A

The design of the service at the time I joined the company looked as follows. Some of its main shortcomings were:

  • Complex navigation consisting of both horizontal and vertical menus, along with a large number of tabs within the pages. Users sometimes do not even know about all the service pages that are located at the third level of nesting.

  • Forms do not automatically save their values. If the user does not click the save button at the end of entering data (the button has no text, only an icon), they will lose their progress.

  • The informational layout of the pages does not have a hierarchy that matches the user tasks. For example, the agent list does not display the status of their activity or the validity of compliance documents, which are the things administrators, the main users of the page—primarily monitor.

  • Poor adaptability on mobile devices, where some pages are impossible to view due to broken layout. Although mobile traffic is not a priority, there are cases where information about a listing needs to be updated quickly when there is no computer at hand, so this shortcoming should have been fixed.

Point B

Navigation

First, we changed the most problematic part—the navigation. Due to the extensive nesting and illogical placement of elements, such as company information, its settings, and users, our clients often contacted support in desperation, unable to find the necessary functions on their own.

In the new navigation, we have combined related elements, reduced the number of main pages, and removed unnecessary nesting.

We moved away from the mix of horizontal and vertical menus with a large number of items to a simple sidebar. We made the background slightly more contrasting and reduced the height of the branded banner.

We implemented a new structure for responsive design. On tablets and mobile devices, the sidebar opens with a button. Please note that there is a slight step effect when transitioning to a phone, which is normal since the margins become slightly smaller.

Developers

On the "Developers" page, there is a list of developer companies with whom brokerage can enter into a partnership sales contract. Developers with whom a partnership contract has been signed are listed on a separate tab. The documents tab contains lists of offers with the terms of the partnership agreements.

New Projects

This page contains a list of new projects from developers with whom the brokerage company has signed a partnership agreement. On this page, the agent looks for projects for their clients, for which they will receive a commission upon closing a deal.

New Project Card

The project card contains all the information about the project from the developer. The agent can select a suitable unit (filters are available in the unit block for this purpose) to make an offer for it or start receiving paid leads for that unit.

Agent Dashboard, Key Metrics

The Agent Dashboard contains information that helps the user generate more leads by improving the quality of their listings. It also includes a sales funnel view, from listing to contract, so the agent can identify opportunities to enhance their performance. The metrics can be compared with top agents in the community where the user operates, which helps in guiding their development direction.

Agent Dashboard, Customer Feedback

The Customer Feedback tab contains non-personalized information about the feedback from agent’s clients. Important metrics here are the overall rating and response rate; the higher these are, the higher the agent’s listings rank in search results.

Agents Insights

On the Agent Insights page, users can see the top agents in the company and the metrics that most significantly impact the company’s revenue. There is detailed information about each agent, structured in a funnel format: first, the metrics of their listings, then the leads they have received, and finally, the deals they have closed.

Users

The Users Page is necessary for monitoring their statuses and activities. If an agent’s compliance documents are about to expire, the admin will see a corresponding warning (along with an email and banner notification).

Users Roles & Permissions

This page lists the roles and their permissions. If the default roles do not meet a user’s needs, they can create a custom role to better suit their business model requirements.

Listings

Page with all client listings. The main tasks a user performs on this page include viewing new lead generation, monitoring quality metrics, searching for duplicates, and taking action for listings that are at risk of being removed from publication.

New Listing

New Listing Creation Form. The user can immediately see how many quality score points they receive for each completed field. This is important because we encourage the most comprehensive listings in the SERPs. The side block moves along with the user’s scroll. Each field change is saved automatically; if a user closes the page before publishing the listing, it will be saved as a draft.

Payment

Standard payment and account top-up page. Saved cards can be managed in the selector, and users can add a new card and save it at their discretion.

My Profile

My Profile is the page with user information. This is an important page because the information here is visible on the agent’s public page in the B2C section of the site, which can be viewed by clicking the "Public Profile" link.

Public Agent Profile

The design of the B2C agent card was also created by a B2B designer, following the B2C team’s guidelines and after a review. It contains information that helps clients choose an agent.

PF Expert Mobile

Our web service is adapted for tablets and mobile devices; however, we also have an app to provide a genuine, high-quality mobile experience. Currently, not all the functionality of the web service is available in the app. Right now, it includes the services that are important for agents, as they are the priority group for whom the app is especially vital. We are continuing to work on adding all the other features.

The Agent Dashboard mirrors the web page in terms of information but has a different layout for more convenient use. Each metric is interactive and opens a new page with detailed information.

The Transactions page lets users confirm a transaction as a completed deal in just two clicks. It makes sense for agents to confirm transactions this way to improve their listings’ positions in search results (as part of our algorithm to encourage the most performing agents) and to record deals in their public track record.

The My Rating page contains information about client feedback.

On the listings page, the agent sees new leads for each listing that they need to contact. The listing details contain important information about the property’s performance and price level relative to the market. The property’s price level is only an estimated parameter from our database; agents know why the current price might be above or below the market, but this parameter, along with performance metrics, helps agents make decisions about adjusting the listing.

The Listing Creation flow largely mirrors the web page but is divided into steps for a more convenient phone experience and uses a different form filling order. First, we ask for property photos so that, based on the metadata, the property address can be suggested automatically, significantly saving time when creating a listing.

Before publishing the listing, the user sees its preview to ensure everything is filled out correctly.

Before publishing, the user selects paid options. Soon, we plan to make this selection smart (the team is working on developing a win-win algorithm), promoting only those listings with the highest potential so the user does not feel that our sole aim is to profit from them.

One of the most frequently used features of the app is Price Finder, which helps determine the most advantageous price for a property based on its parameters and location. This feature is extremely important for agents in negotiations with owners and for making decisions.

The Lead card is enriched with client property search preferences, which we gather from their search history on our website. This helps the agent to be more targeted in addressing the lead’s needs.
